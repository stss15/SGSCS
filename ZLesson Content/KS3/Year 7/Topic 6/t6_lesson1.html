Binary Numbers: How Computers Talk

Have you ever wondered how computers, phones, and tablets understand what we want them to do? [cite: 1] It's actually pretty amazing—they talk using a secret language called binary. [cite: 2] Binary might sound complicated at first, but it's simply made up of two numbers: 0 and 1. Imagine these numbers like tiny switches that can turn things ON or OFF. [cite: 3] When these switches work together, they help computers make logical decisions and process data quickly and accurately. [cite: 4]

Why Binary?

Computers need a clear and simple way to understand instructions and store information. [cite: 5] They are packed full of tiny electronic switches that can either be in an ON (1) or OFF (0) state. [cite: 6] Using just two states makes it straightforward for computers to follow logical instructions and process data efficiently. [cite: 7] Binary is perfect for computers because it’s simple, clear, and reliable, allowing them to perform incredibly complex tasks by breaking them down into simple processes. [cite: 8]

The Magic Behind Binary

To understand how binary works, think of it like a special code. [cite: 9] Each digit in binary, called a 'bit', has a specific value. [cite: 10] Bits are grouped together, usually in groups of eight, called bytes. [cite: 11] Each bit within the byte has a different value: [cite: 12]

128 | 64 | 32 | 16 | 8 | 4 | 2 | 1 [cite: 12, 13]

These values double each time, starting from the right. [cite: 13] This doubling is key to how binary helps computers logically organize and process data. [cite: 14]

Turning Binary into Numbers We Understand

Let’s decode the binary number 10110101 step-by-step: [cite: 15]

128 | 64 | 32 | 16 | 8 | 4 | 2 | 1 [cite: 16]

1 | 0 | 1 | 1 | 0 | 1 | 0 | 1 [cite: 17]

We add up the values wherever there's a 1: [cite: 17]

128 + 32 + 16 + 4 + 1 = 181 [cite: 17]

So, the binary 10110101 logically represents the number 181. [cite: 17]

Why Use Binary Instead of Regular Numbers?

Regular numbers (denary numbers), like 10, 20, or 100, use ten digits (0–9). [cite: 18] But computers find it difficult to handle ten different values at once. [cite: 19] Using binary simplifies this, making it quicker and easier to process data, helping computers run smoothly and efficiently. [cite: 20]

Turning Regular Numbers into Binary

Converting regular (denary) numbers into binary helps you understand how computers logically organize data. [cite: 21] Let’s take the number 45 and see how this works: [cite: 22]

  • First, find the largest binary value that fits into 45 without going over: that's 32. [cite: 22]
  • Subtract 32 from 45, leaving 13. [cite: 22]
  • The next biggest binary number that fits into 13 is 8. Subtract it, leaving 5. [cite: 22]
  • Now subtract 4, leaving 1. [cite: 22]
  • Finally subtract 1, leaving 0. [cite: 22]

Placing these values into our chart: [cite: 22]

128 | 64 | 32 | 16 | 8 | 4 | 2 | 1 [cite: 23]

0 | 0 | 1 | 0 | 1 | 1 | 0 | 1 [cite: 24]

The binary number for 45 is 00101101. [cite: 24]

Another Cool Trick: Division by 2

You can also convert denary numbers into binary by repeatedly dividing by two and keeping track of remainders. [cite: 25] Let's logically process this method using the number 22: [cite: 25]

  • 22 ÷ 2 = 11, remainder 0 [cite: 25]
  • 11 ÷ 2 = 5, remainder 1 [cite: 25]
  • 5 ÷ 2 = 2, remainder 1 [cite: 25]
  • 2 ÷ 2 = 1, remainder 0 [cite: 25]
  • 1 ÷ 2 = 0, remainder 1 [cite: 25]

Reading backwards, you get 10110. To fill up a byte, add zeros to the front: 00010110. [cite: 25]
